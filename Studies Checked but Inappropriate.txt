# STUDIES CHECKED BUT INAPPROPRIATE
#
# Studies that were investigated but are NOT many-analyst studies.
# Keep them here so we never re-check them.
#
# Format (one per block):
#   <Author(s) (Year) - Title>
#       REASON: <why it is not a many-analyst study, e.g. multiverse / replication
#               project / commentary / methodology / re-analysis of an existing
#               many-analyst dataset>
# ============================================================================

Holzmeister et al. (2024) - Heterogeneity in Effect Size Estimates - PNAS 121(32)
    REASON: Synthesis/framework paper (by the Innsbruck/SSE group) that
            partitions heterogeneity into population/design/analytical components
            using data from EXISTING crowd-sourced/many-analyst studies. It is
            not itself a single new many-analyst data collection. (Its component
            datasets, e.g. Nonstandard Errors and the 45-team competition study,
            are tracked separately.)

Sulik et al. (2023) - Why Do Scientists Disagree? A Study of the Psychological Sciences - PsyArXiv (10.31234/osf.io/9bd8k)
    REASON: An empirical study ABOUT the causes of scientific disagreement
            (surveying scientists), not a many-analyst design in which multiple
            teams analyze the same dataset. Cited by the review as background.

Soebhag, Van Vliet & Verwijmeren (2024) - Non-standard Errors in Asset Pricing: Mind Your Sorts - J Empirical Finance 78
    REASON: MULTIVERSE / specification analysis by a SINGLE team (3 authors)
            who themselves data-mine 250+ factor-construction versions. Not
            multiple independent analyst teams. (Title echoes Menkveld's
            "Nonstandard Errors" but the design is multiverse, not many-analyst.)

Sarafoglou et al. (2024) - Subjective Evidence Evaluation Survey for Many-Analysts Studies - Royal Society Open Science 11(7):240125
    REASON: METHODOLOGY paper. Introduces a survey instrument (SEES) to be USED
            within many-analyst studies to capture analysts' subjective reasoning.
            It is a tool, not itself a many-analyst data collection.

"Many AI Analysts, One Dataset: Navigating the Agentic Data Science Multiverse" (2026) - arXiv 2602.18710
    REASON: Uses AI/LLM agents (not teams of human analysts) as the "analysts".
            An AI analog of the many-analyst design; out of scope for this human
            many-analyst contact list. (Related AI-analyst papers: Grundl 2026,
            McCully 2026 - same exclusion.)

Delios et al. (2022) - Examining the Generalizability of Research Findings from Archival Data - PNAS 119(30)
    REASON: A reproduction/generalizability project: one coordinating team repeats
            29 strategic-management effects in direct reproductions and in novel
            time periods/geographies (plus a forecasting survey). This is mass
            reproduction/generalizability (many DIFFERENT effects), not many
            independent teams analyzing the SAME dataset/task.

Rohrer, Davey Smith & Munafo (2025) - What Can Be Learned When Multiple Analysts Arrive at Different Estimates - European Journal of Epidemiology
    REASON: Commentary/editorial discussing the many-analyst approach (accompanying
            Kowall et al.'s epidemiology multi-analyst study). Not an original
            many-analyst data collection.

Aczel et al. (2021) - Consensus-based Guidance for Conducting and Reporting Multi-Analyst Studies - eLife 10:e72185
    REASON: METHODOLOGY / guidance paper on how to conduct & report many-analyst
            studies. Not itself a many-analyst data collection.

Kummerfeld & Jones (2023) - One Data Set, Many Analysts: Implications for Practicing Scientists - Frontiers in Psychology
    REASON: Perspective/implications paper discussing the many-analyst phenomenon.
            Not an original many-analyst study.

MAPS - Mapping the Analytical PathS of a Crowdsourced Data Analysis (OSF buqk8)
    REASON: Not a separate study. It is the DataExplained analytic-path mapping of
            the SAME crowdsourced dataset already counted as Schweinsberg et al.
            (2021) "Same Data, Different Conclusions". Duplicate / secondary output.

Norgaard et al. (2019) - Preprocessing, Prediction and Significance: Framework and Application to Brain Imaging - MICCAI 2019
    REASON: A methodological framework / multiverse-style pipeline analysis (single
            team systematically varying preprocessing choices), not many independent
            analyst teams. Listed by the source doc under "methodological perspectives."

Breznau (2021) - I Saw You in the Crowd: Credibility, Reproducibility, and Meta-Utility - PS: Political Science & Politics
    REASON: Commentary / methodological perspective on crowdsourced research, not a
            many-analyst data collection.

Auspurg & Bruderl (2021) - Has the Credibility of the Social Sciences Been Credibly Destroyed? Reanalyzing the "Many Analysts, One Data Set" Project - Socius
    REASON: Re-analysis / commentary on the Silberzahn et al. (2018) dataset. The
            underlying many-analyst study is already tracked; this is not a new one.

Silberzahn & Uhlmann (2015) - Crowdsourced Research: Many Hands Make Tight Work - Nature (Comment)
    REASON: Magazine comment/perspective introducing the crowdsourcing-analysis idea,
            not a many-analyst study itself.

Uhlmann et al. (2019) - Scientific Utopia III: Crowdsourcing Science - Perspectives on Psychological Science
    REASON: Perspective/agenda paper on crowdsourcing science, not a many-analyst
            data collection.

Wagenmakers, Sarafoglou & Aczel (2022a) - One Statistical Analysis Must Not Rule Them All - Nature (Comment)
    REASON: Commentary advocating many-analyst approaches; not an original study.

Wagenmakers, Sarafoglou & Aczel (2022b) - Facing the Unknown Unknowns of Data Analysis
    REASON: Commentary / methodological perspective; not an original many-analyst study.

Many Speech Analyses (MSA) - many-speech-analyses.github.io
    REASON: Not a separate study. The MSA project IS the one published as Coretta
            et al. (2023) "Multidimensional Signals and Analytic Flexibility"
            (already in Contact Info.xlsx). Duplicate.
